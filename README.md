The core idea is that this project is not a game in the traditional sense, but a formal language for describing hypothetical magic—a programming language for reality itself. Spells are inert pieces of information, comparable to source code written on paper. They have no cost, consume no resources, and produce no effects until they are executed by a trigger that injects mana into them. The trigger's only purpose is to begin execution by supplying a small amount of startup mana, analogous to booting a computer program. Once running, a spell may continue operating by converting external forms of energy into mana through explicit conversion subroutines, but these converters themselves require the initial mana pulse to activate, preventing free perpetual magic. Mana is therefore the universal input accepted by every spell, while all other forms of energy—heat, electricity, sunlight, chemical energy, kinetic energy, and so on—must first be converted into mana before the spell can use them. Likewise, spells can convert mana back into any other form of energy, but they never violate the conservation of energy or matter; they merely change how energy is transferred. In this system, the official definition of magic is any chain of events that lacks a physical causal line. Magic does not ignore physics or create energy from nothing; instead, it replaces the physical mechanism that would normally connect cause and effect with a direct informational one. A rock levitating is magical not because energy appears from nowhere, but because the required energy is supplied through mana and the effect occurs without any physical force or intermediary acting on the rock. This is because the universe itself is fundamentally informational—an "it from bit" reality—where matter, energy, and physical laws emerge from an underlying computational substrate. The magical language is therefore not inventing arbitrary symbols but expressing instructions that ultimately resolve to this substrate. At the deepest level, reality operates on an unimaginably low-level informational language analogous to machine code or binary. Human magicians never write this directly. Instead, they write spells in a higher-level symbolic language with a formal grammar, comparable to a programming language, where every symbol, ring, connection, and geometric relationship has a precise semantic purpose and nothing is decorative. The circular sigil is therefore not artwork but a structured diagram optimized for expressing relationships, dependencies, scopes, shared mana sources, conditions, reusable subroutines, and execution flow—more like a circuit board or software architecture diagram than a mystical glyph. The language should be expressive enough that, in principle, the entire spell could be written in plain English while remaining functionally identical; symbols exist purely because they are more compact and practical. Each spell contains a legend defining what its chosen symbols represent, allowing every magician to invent their own notation while preserving the same underlying semantics. One mage may use English words, another geometric symbols, another an entirely original alphabet, just as mathematicians and programmers use different variable names and notation. The legend serves as human-readable documentation and can be included directly in the spell so that anyone can interpret it, or omitted to obscure the spell's meaning. Ultimately, the universe does not recognize English, symbols, or artistic sigils—it recognizes the underlying informational references encoded by the spell. The legend simply records which human-readable names correspond to those references. Over time, magicians naturally build libraries of reusable subroutines, abstractions, converters, and higher-level constructs, allowing increasingly sophisticated spells to be composed from simpler components, just as software engineers build complex programs from functions and modules. A simple spell might only describe a single action, while an immense spell could combine hundreds of growth, irrigation, terrain shaping, stoneworking, environmental control, and triggering routines into one enormous sigil that resembles an integrated circuit. The intellectual challenge and enjoyment come not from memorizing predefined spells, but from mastering a complete formal language whose grammar is expressive enough to describe any magical effect that obeys the world's physical laws, making the project effectively a complete programming language whose fictional runtime is the universe itself.
